Imo shochu is distilled from satsumaimo — the sweet potato that gives Satsuma (the old name for Kagoshima) its name. The spirit is made only in Kagoshima, and the tradition predates Scotch whisky by at least a century.

It is not sake. It is not soju. It is its own thing, with a flavor profile that rewards patience: earthy and slightly funky when young, smoother and more complex with age, capable of producing single-batch expressions that stand alongside any fine spirit in the world.

## How it's made

The process begins with a koji mold culture (usually rice koji) that converts starches to fermentable sugars. Sweet potatoes are cooked and combined with water and the koji culture in a fermentation tank. After fermentation, the mash is distilled — traditionally in a pot still, which preserves more of the potato character than column distillation.

Most imo shochu is bottled at 25% ABV (regular strength) or 35% (full strength), though small-batch expressions can reach higher.

## How to drink it

**Oyuwari** (hot water): Add hot water at roughly 6:4 shochu to water, or adjust to taste. The heat opens up the aroma significantly. This is the most common way Kagoshima locals drink it.

**Mizuwari** (cold water): Room temperature or cold water, same ratio. Lighter and more refreshing.

**On the rocks (rokkusu):** Works well for higher-quality, aged expressions.

**Straight:** Only the best bottles, at room temperature.

Avoid mixing imo shochu with juice or soda — it obscures the point. The flavors are subtle enough to lose.

## Distilleries to visit

**Nishi Shuzo** in Hioki produces Taisei, one of the most awarded imo shochus in Japan. Tours and tastings available on weekdays; advance booking required.

**Sankosha** (Three Happiness) in Hioki focuses on traditional stone-pot distillation. The resulting spirit is rough-edged and deeply satisfying. Their Mori Izo label has a cult following.

**Kirishima Shuzo** in Kirishima city is the largest producer and the easiest to visit. Free tours, substantial tasting bar, good introduction to the range of styles.

## What to buy

At liquor shops in Kagoshima City (Yamada Denki Liquors and the specialty shops around Tenmonkan), you'll find expressions unavailable elsewhere in Japan. Aged bottles (koshu) in ceramic bottles make the best gifts and the best drinking.

Budget 1,500–5,000 yen for a 720ml bottle of something genuinely good. The top-shelf stuff goes higher.
