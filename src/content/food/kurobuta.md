Kurobuta (黒豚, "black pig") is the Berkshire breed as raised in Kagoshima, where the tradition of keeping these black pigs dates back over 400 years. The Satsuma domain used sweet potato pulp — a byproduct of imo shochu production — as pig feed, which influenced the fat quality and flavor profile over generations.

The result is pork with more intense flavor, finer marbling, and a sweetness absent in commodity pork. It's the reason Kagoshima shabu-shabu is worth seeking out specifically, not just as any Japanese hot pot.

## What to order

**Shabu-shabu** is the premier preparation. Thin-sliced kurobuta belly and loin are briefly swirled in a light kombu dashi broth, then dipped in ponzu or sesame sauce. The fat renders at temperature in a way industrial pork doesn't — each slice has a richness that doesn't feel heavy.

**Tonkatsu** (breaded cutlet) is the other benchmark. Kagoshima-style tends toward thicker cuts than Tokyo tonkatsu, with the fat cap left on. The Tenmonkan area has several long-running specialists.

**Kurobuta ramen** appears on menus across the city. The broth is pork-bone based; quality varies widely. The best bowls are in smaller neighborhood shops rather than tourist-facing restaurants.

## Where to eat

**Kumasotei** in the Tenmonkan district is the most frequently recommended shabu-shabu specialist. Long-running, formal-ish, excellent quality. Expect to spend 3,500–6,000 yen per person for dinner.

**Tonkatsu Hamakatu** has multiple locations and a reliable kitchen. Better for a quick tonkatsu lunch than a special dinner.

For a more casual approach, the depachika (department store basement) food halls at Yamakataya and Maruya Gardens both have kurobuta bento and prepared foods that show the quality without the sit-down price.

## The Kagoshima brand

The designation "Kagoshima kurobuta" requires the pigs to be pure Berkshire breed, raised in Kagoshima Prefecture, for a minimum period with specific feed standards. Imitations exist elsewhere in Japan — if the menu says "Berkshire-style" or "kuro-breed" without the Kagoshima provenance, it may not be the same animal.

In Kagoshima itself, this is generally not a concern — the local pride in the product is genuine, and shops display the certification freely.
