Shirokuma (白熊, "white bear") is a Kagoshima invention. A bowl — more accurately a small mountain — of finely shaved ice, condensed milk, and toppings that typically include canned fruit, azuki red beans, mochi, and sometimes kiwi or mandarin orange. The name comes from the white bear face sometimes drawn in the toppings.

It was created at Mujaki, a sweets shop in Tenmonkan, in 1947. The shop still operates. The dessert spread across Japan from here.

## Where to eat it

**Mujaki** (天文館むじゃき) is the original. The Tenmonkan location has been operating since 1947 and the basement floor is dedicated to shirokuma in numerous variations. Expect queues on summer weekends. The classic version is the correct starting point before exploring variations.

**Convenience store shirokuma:** Seven-Eleven and Lawson sell packaged shirokuma year-round, and the Kagoshima versions (produced locally for the Kagoshima market) are genuinely good as a backup or late-night option.

Most kissaten (coffee shops) and dessert shops in the Tenmonkan area serve some version of it. Quality varies; Mujaki remains the standard.

## When to eat it

Summer in Kagoshima is hot and humid — the city averages around 32°C in August, with high humidity from its coastal position. Shirokuma in these conditions is a practical necessity, not a luxury.

But the dessert is sold year-round. Mujaki's winter version uses different toppings (hot azuki rather than fruit); some regulars prefer it.

## Beyond Mujaki

The Kagoshima shaved ice tradition extends beyond shirokuma. A number of newer specialty shops have appeared in the city in recent years, serving kakigori (traditional shaved ice) with seasonal fruit syrups and house-made condensed milk. Tenmonkan and Kagoshima-Chuo area have the highest concentration.

The difference between machine-shaved ice and hand-cranked kakigori is significant — the latter produces fluffier, less dense shards that absorb syrup differently. If you encounter a specialist shop offering hand-shaved kakigori, try it alongside the Mujaki original.
